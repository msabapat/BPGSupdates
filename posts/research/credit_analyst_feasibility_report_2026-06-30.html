Beaver Univariate Ratios Beaver · 1966 Single-ratio cutoffs — the ancestor of the field; useful only as a baseline.
RUN NOW⌄
What it does

Tests 30 individual financial ratios one at a time for their ability to separate failed from non-failed firms using a simple cutoff rule. Each ratio is evaluated by the misclassification rate at its optimal threshold. The best single predictor found: cash flow / total debt. Beaver tracked ratios for up to five years before failure and found systematic deterioration.

Why it works

Proved for the first time that financial statement data contains extractable predictive information about failure, and that the signal strengthens as you approach default. Cash flow / total debt works because a firm that generates insufficient operating cash flow relative to its debt burden is structurally unsustainable — it must refinance, sell assets, or default.

Shortcomings
  • Not a model — just a ratio-screening study. No probability output.
  • Single-ratio ignores correlations: a firm can have great cash flow but catastrophic leverage simultaneously.
  • Binary cutoff is fragile; no grey zone.
  • Immediately superseded by Altman's MDA framework (1968).

Altman Z-score Altman · 1968 · Public manufacturers Five-ratio linear discriminant score. Distress zone < 1.81, safe zone > 2.99.
RUN NOW⌄
Z = 1.2·(WC/TA) + 1.4·(RE/TA) + 3.3·(EBIT/TA) + 0.6·(MVE/TL) + 0.999·(Sales/TA)
What it does

Multiple discriminant analysis (MDA) finds the linear combination of five ratios that maximally separates bankrupt from non-bankrupt firms in training data. Each ratio covers a distinct failure dimension: liquidity (WC/TA), cumulative profitability (RE/TA — older firms accumulate more), operating efficiency (EBIT/TA), leverage (MVE/TL using market cap), and asset turnover (Sales/TA).

Why it works

MDA maximizes the ratio of between-group variance to within-group variance — it finds the single axis in 5-dimensional ratio space along which bankrupt and healthy firms are most separated. The Z-score acts as a sufficient statistic for this axis. The combination of liquidity, profitability, leverage, and efficiency captures the core failure dynamics: firms default when operating losses erode equity, leverage becomes unsustainable, and liquidity dries up simultaneously.

Shortcomings
  • Original sample: 33 bankrupt + 33 matched non-bankrupt manufacturers, 1946–1965. Tiny and 60+ years old.
  • LDA assumes multivariate normality and equal covariance matrices — rarely satisfied by financial ratios.
  • Sales/TA is industry-sensitive: high for retailers, low for capital-intensive firms → biased in cross-industry settings.
  • Grey zone (1.81–2.99) has high misclassification rate; the model gives no guidance there.
  • No probability output — only a score index. Cannot compute EL = PD × LGD.
  • Published coefficients should be re-estimated; multiple studies document significant drift over decades.

Altman Z′ and Z″-scores Altman · 1983 (Z′) · 1995 (Z″) · Private firms / Non-manufacturers Z′ replaces market equity with book equity (for private firms). Z″ removes Sales/TA entirely (for cross-industry use).
RUN NOW⌄
Z″ = 3.25 + 6.56·(WC/TA) + 3.26·(RE/TA) + 6.72·(EBIT/TA) + 1.05·(BVE/TL)
What it does

Z′ makes the original Z-score applicable to private companies by replacing market value of equity (MVE) with book value of equity (BVE) in the leverage ratio, since private firms have no observable market cap. Z″ goes further: it drops the Sales/TA turnover term completely (making it more industry-neutral) and recalibrates. Z″ thresholds: distress < 1.1, safe > 2.6.

Why it works

Sales/TA systematically varies by industry structure rather than by credit health — a grocery chain has 3× the asset turnover of a steel mill at the same credit quality. Removing it reduces false signals in a pooled cross-industry dataset. The 3.25 constant shifts the score distribution so zones are comparably calibrated to the original. Same MDA rationale otherwise.

Shortcomings
  • Book equity is a worse leverage signal than market equity — backward-looking, doesn't update continuously.
  • Dropping Sales/TA loses real information even if it reduces industry bias.
  • LDA assumptions still apply.
  • Zone thresholds vary across publications — use Z″ only as a relative score, not the absolute zones.

Ohlson O-score Ohlson · 1980 Nine-factor logit — first model to produce a genuine probability output between 0 and 1.
RUN NOW⌄
O = −1.32 − 0.407·SIZE + 6.03·TLTA − 1.43·WCTA + 0.076·CLCA − 1.72·OENEG − 2.37·NITA − 1.83·FUTL + 0.285·INTWO − 0.521·CHIN PD = 1 / (1 + e^(−O))
What it does

Nine-variable logit regression on a panel of 105 bankrupt and 2,058 non-bankrupt firms from Compustat, 1970–1976. Variables: SIZE (log total assets deflated by GNP price level), TLTA (leverage), WCTA (liquidity), CLCA (short-term liquidity), OENEG (dummy: liabilities > assets), NITA (ROA), FUTL (cash flow from operations / total liabilities), INTWO (dummy: two consecutive loss years), CHIN (change in net income — earnings momentum).

Why it works

Logit produces a proper probability via the sigmoid transform — unlike Z-score, you can interpret it as P(default within 1 year). Ohlson used a far larger and more representative sample than Altman (not match-paired). The inclusion of flow variables (FUTL, NITA) alongside stock variables (TLTA, WCTA) and qualitative dummies (OENEG, INTWO) covers multiple dimensions of distress simultaneously, capturing both the level and trend of financial health.

Shortcomings
  • 1980 coefficients are 45 years old; performance degrades substantially vs re-estimated versions.
  • SIZE term needs a GNP/GDP price deflator (FRED series GDPDEF) — one extra data pull.
  • Like Altman, backward-looking; no market-price signal.
  • Logit assumes log-linear relationship between log-odds and each predictor — doesn't capture interactions automatically.
  • OENEG (liabilities > assets) is a very late-stage flag; firms rarely reach this point before other signals fire.

Zmijewski X-score Zmijewski · 1984 Three-variable probit — the most parsimonious full-probability model. Clean, fast, easy to explain.
RUN NOW⌄
X = −4.336 − 4.513·ROA + 5.679·(TotalDebt/TotalAssets) − 0.004·(CurrentAssets/CurrentLiabilities) PD = Φ(X) [normal CDF]
What it does

Probit model distilling credit risk to three ratios: ROA (net income / assets — profitability), leverage (total debt / total assets), and liquidity (current assets / current liabilities). Used a choice-based sampling correction to address the class-imbalance problem (defaults are rare). Output is a probit probability via the standard normal CDF.

Why it works

These three variables capture the essential triad of credit risk: can the firm generate earnings to service debt? (ROA), how much debt does it carry? (leverage), can it meet near-term obligations? (liquidity). The choice-based sampling correction addresses a key flaw in earlier models — sampling equal bankrupt/non-bankrupt observations inflates the apparent precision of coefficient estimates.

Shortcomings
  • Only 3 variables — real credit risk has more dimensions; important signals (earnings trend, cash flow quality) are omitted.
  • ROA is a point-in-time snapshot; doesn't capture whether profitability is improving or deteriorating.
  • No market-price information whatsoever.
  • Probit vs logit is largely a theoretical distinction; empirically the performance difference is negligible.
Your data

All three inputs directly available: roa, de (debt/equity), currentratio. Or compute from primitives: netinc/assets, debt/assets, assetsc/liabilitiesc. Simplest full-probability model to batch-score your entire universe today.

Shumway Hazard Model Shumway · 2001 Discrete-time hazard (panel logit across all firm-years) — fixes the survivorship bias baked into static models.
RUN NOW*⌄
What it does

Instead of fitting a logit on a single cross-section of firms, fits a logit on all firm-year observations — including years before bankruptcy. The hazard h(t) = P(default in year t | survived to t) is modeled as a logit function of time-varying covariates: 3 accounting ratios (net income/assets, total liabilities/assets, working capital/assets) plus 2 market variables (relative market size, prior-year excess return). Uses published coefficients from the 2001 paper.

Why it works

Static models (Altman, Ohlson) typically fit on matched cross-sections — one observation per firm (the last available before failure). This implicitly conditions on survival until the estimation window, introducing survivorship bias into coefficient estimates. Using all firm-years exploits the full information in the longitudinal panel. The market variables (size, excess return) capture real-time information that accounting ratios miss between filing dates.

Shortcomings
  • Annual frequency — misses within-year deterioration; Chava-Jarrow (monthly) improves this.
  • Conditional independence across firm-years is assumed but violated — serial correlation in credit quality is strong.
  • Published 2001 coefficients need re-estimation for the current era; market microstructure has changed significantly.
  • * RUN NOW with published coefficients as a rough ranking; label-gate for re-estimation.

Campbell–Hilscher–Szilagyi (CHS) Campbell, Hilscher, Szilagyi · 2008 · Journal of Finance Dynamic logit with 8 market-adjusted predictors — the modern academic standard for public-firm PD.
RUN NOW*⌄
PD = logit(β·NIMTA + β·TLMTA + β·EXRET + β·SIGMA + β·RSIZE + β·CASHMTA + β·MB + β·PRICE) where NIMTA = net income / (equity_mkt + liabilities); TLMTA = liabilities / (equity_mkt + liabilities)
What it does

Monthly-updated dynamic logit on 8 predictors: NIMTA (net income / market total assets — ROA with market denominator), TLMTA (total liabilities / MTA — leverage that responds instantly when equity falls), EXRET (excess return over S&P — recent momentum), SIGMA (3-month equity volatility — uncertainty), RSIZE (log of firm mcap / S&P mcap — size), CASHMTA (cash/MTA — liquidity), MB (market-to-book), PRICE (log price capped at $15 — penny-stock signal). Each predictor is geometrically-weighted over prior months, emphasizing recent observations.

Why it works

Using market total assets (equity_mkt + liabilities) as the denominator means NIMTA and TLMTA respond immediately when equity price falls — they don't wait for the next quarterly filing. A firm losing half its market cap sees its TLMTA double even before any accounting change. The geometric weighting captures the trend, not just the level. Including SIGMA and PRICE captures tail-risk signals that pure ratio models miss: high volatility and penny-stock status are empirically strong predictors independent of leverage.

Shortcomings
  • Requires live equity prices — no private firms.
  • The distress anomaly: firms with high CHS-predicted PD earn anomalously low subsequent returns, inconsistent with distress being a compensated risk factor. Do not use high CHS-PD as a buy signal — it has historically lost money.
  • Dynamic logit (not a true survival model) — technically treats each monthly observation as independent, which understates standard errors.
  • Original coefficients estimated on 1963–2003 data; market structure (HFT, passive investing) has changed; re-estimation recommended.

Merton Structural Model Merton · 1974 Equity as a call option on firm assets. Distance-to-default → PD via the normal CDF. The theoretical foundation of all structural models.
RUN NOW⌄
E = V·N(d₁) − F·e^(−rT)·N(d₂) d₁ = [ln(V/F) + (r + σ_V²/2)·T] / (σ_V·√T) d₂ = d₁ − σ_V·√T PD = N(−d₂) = N(−DD) where DD = d₂ is distance-to-default
What it does

Models firm value V as a geometric Brownian motion. Equity E is the residual claim — a European call option on V with strike F (face value of debt) and maturity T (debt horizon). Using Black-Scholes-Merton, observe equity price and equity volatility (σ_E) and solve iteratively for the unobservable asset value (V) and asset volatility (σ_V). The distance-to-default DD = d₂ measures how many standard deviations of asset returns separate current assets from the default threshold. PD = N(−DD).

Why it works

The equity market is forward-looking — it incorporates expectations about future earnings, asset values, and risks that haven't yet appeared in financial statements. As a firm deteriorates, equity price falls, equity volatility rises, and both forces simultaneously increase leverage and reduce the distance-to-default. The option-pricing framework provides a theoretically grounded conversion from observable equity signals to credit risk, with a direct link between the two through the firm's capital structure.

Shortcomings
  • Default only at debt maturity T — not realistic (firms default at any time due to covenant breaches, cash shortfalls). Black-Cox addresses this.
  • Lognormal asset dynamics → thin tails; real default frequencies exceed N(−DD) predictions (especially for high-DD firms). Hence KMV's empirical mapping.
  • Iterative solve is computationally intensive for 4,000+ names daily; Bharath-Shumway's closed-form gets 90% of the predictive content with zero iteration.
  • Debt structure simplified to face value F; doesn't handle convertibles, covenants, revolvers, or off-balance-sheet liabilities.
  • Sensitive to choice of T (debt maturity, typically set to 1 year for current + 5 years for long-term — arbitrary).

Black-Cox First-Passage Model Black & Cox · 1976 Default the first time assets hit a barrier — more realistic than Merton's single-date default.
RUN NOW⌄
What it does

Extends Merton by triggering default at the first passage time τ = inf{t : V_t ≤ H_t}, where H_t = H·e^(−γ(T−t)) is an exponentially decaying barrier. This allows default at any point in [0,T], not just at maturity. The survival probability has a closed-form involving the first-passage distribution of a drifted Brownian motion. PD over horizon T is larger than Merton's PD for the same inputs because any path that hits the barrier counts as a default.

Why it works

Bond covenants and debt agreements allow creditors to declare default — or demand immediate repayment — any time the firm's condition falls below certain thresholds. A firm can run out of cash long before its bonds mature. The first-passage framework captures this economic reality: default is a path-dependent event that can happen anytime assets deteriorate enough, not just on a fixed future date. It's the continuous-time equivalent of a firm becoming technically insolvent.

Shortcomings
  • The barrier H must be specified — typically a fraction of face value of debt, but this is arbitrary and sensitive.
  • Closed-form solutions require constant (or exponentially decaying) barriers; stochastic barriers require Monte Carlo.
  • More parameterization complexity than Merton with limited incremental PD scoring accuracy in practice.
  • More commonly used in credit derivative pricing than in PD scoring; Merton + empirical mapping (KMV) dominates in practice.
Your data

Same inputs as Merton, plus a barrier assumption (H typically = 50–70% of face value of debt). Implementable but low priority for the scoring application. More useful if you ever build a credit spread term structure.

KMV / Expected Default Frequency (EDF) Moody's Analytics (KMV) · commercial, ~1990s Empirical Merton: maps distance-to-default to observed default frequencies rather than the normal CDF — fixes Merton's fat-tail problem.
NEEDS LABEL⌄
What it does

Computes distance-to-default DD exactly as Merton, but instead of applying the normal CDF to get PD = N(−DD), maps DD to an empirically-observed default frequency using Moody's proprietary database of millions of firm-years and hundreds of thousands of observed defaults globally. If DD = 2.0, the EDF is the actual fraction of all firms with DD ≈ 2.0 that defaulted within 1 year in the database. KMV's "default point" also differs: set at current liabilities + 0.5 × long-term debt (not face value), a practically calibrated threshold.

Why it works

The standard normal CDF N(−DD) systematically understates empirical default frequencies, especially for DD in the 1–3 range where most near-default firms sit — real asset-return distributions have fat tails. The empirical mapping corrects this by grounding predictions in observed reality. KMV's database size (global, multi-decade) gives robust estimates across the full DD range, including thin tail regions where small samples would fail.

Shortcomings
  • The empirical DD→EDF mapping is entirely proprietary — you can compute DD but cannot apply KMV's specific table without a Moody's Analytics subscription (~$50K+/yr).
  • Global database includes diverse jurisdictions; may not be well-calibrated specifically for US small-cap universe.
  • Inherits all Merton structural limitations (lognormal assets, point-in-time leverage only).

Bharath–Shumway Naïve Distance-to-Default Bharath & Shumway · 2008 Closed-form Merton proxy — no iterative solve, ~90% of full Merton's predictive power. Ideal for batch automation. Validated live on your database.
RUN NOW⌄
σ_V ≈ (E/(E+F))·σ_E + (F/(E+F))·(0.05 + 0.25·σ_E) DD_naive = [ln((E+F)/F) + (μ − σ_V²/2)] / σ_V PD_naive = N(−DD_naive) where E = marketcap_ffill, F = debtc + 0.5·debtnc, σ_E = vol_90d, μ = mom_12m1m
What it does

Replaces the Merton iterative solve with closed-form approximations: asset value ≈ E + F (total market value of the firm), and asset volatility ≈ a leverage-weighted blend of equity volatility and an approximated debt volatility (0.05 + 0.25·σ_E). Inserts these into the standard DD formula. Despite the approximation, Bharath and Shumway (2008) demonstrate this captures ~90% of the predictive content of the full Merton model in hazard regressions.

Why it works

The dominant signal in Merton DD is the ratio of asset value to debt (the numerator of DD) and asset volatility (the denominator). The precise BSM asset-value extraction via iterative solve adds refinement but not the core signal. The closed-form approximation captures both primary drivers efficiently. The paper's key insight: the functional form of DD matters more than the precise solution for asset value and volatility.

Shortcomings
  • By construction, slightly less precise than full Merton — the ~10% foregone is real, just not enough to justify the iterative solve in batch processing.
  • PD = N(−DD) still suffers from fat-tail understatement (same as Merton); use for ranking rather than calibrated probability.
  • Uses prior-year stock return as expected asset return μ — very noisy signal.
  • Critical gotcha: vol_90d in your DB is already annualized (~0.45 for Ford). Do NOT multiply by √252 — this was validated on your live data.

CreditGrades Model RiskMetrics / JPMorgan / Goldman Sachs / Deutsche Bank · 2002 Structural model with an uncertain default barrier — built to track CDS spreads, not just score PD.
PARTIAL⌄
What it does

Extends the Black-Cox first-passage model by making the default barrier itself random: L_bar ~ Lognormal(λ, λ²), representing uncertainty about exactly when covenants or cash constraints would force a default event. Produces closed-form credit spread curves as a function of equity price, equity vol, leverage, and the barrier uncertainty parameter λ. Specifically designed by four major banks to match observable CDS spread behavior.

Why it works

The deterministic-barrier Merton model produces near-zero spreads for low-leverage firms at short maturities — empirically wrong, since even investment-grade firms trade at non-zero CDS spreads. The uncertain barrier creates a "jump-to-default" component that matches short-dated spread levels. The model was calibrated to match observed CDS spread dynamics across the credit spectrum.

Shortcomings
  • Built as a spread model — requires CDS or bond data to calibrate λ for each name; without that, λ must be assumed.
  • Without spread data, can compute a PD-like output but it won't be market-calibrated.
  • More complex than Bharath-Shumway with limited incremental PD accuracy without the spread inputs.

Leland / Leland-Toft Endogenous Barrier Leland · 1994 · Leland & Toft · 1996 Equityholders choose when to default — the barrier is endogenous to optimal capital structure, not set by the modeler.
PARTIAL / LOW PRIORITY⌄
What it does

In Black-Cox, the default barrier H is specified exogenously by the modeler. Leland derives H endogenously: equityholders choose the asset value threshold at which they optimally surrender the firm to debtholders (maximizing equity value), incorporating the tax shield of debt coupon payments and the deadweight costs of bankruptcy. Leland-Toft (1996) extends this to finite-maturity debt. The model simultaneously determines optimal leverage and the default boundary.

Why it works

Real equityholders behave strategically: they will continue servicing debt as long as equity remains in-the-money (option value), but surrender when assets fall below the point where the firm is worth more dead than alive. The endogenous barrier captures this strategic default decision without an arbitrary threshold assumption. The model also produces testable predictions about optimal capital structure.

Shortcomings
  • Requires calibration of bankruptcy cost fraction α and tax shield parameter — both unobservable.
  • Primarily a capital structure theory model, not a credit scoring tool; empirical PD accuracy is not its strength.
  • Complex numerical solution (Leland-Toft involves integrating over maturity structure).
  • Low incremental accuracy over Merton/Bharath-Shumway for cross-sectional default prediction.
Your data

Implementable with equity price, vol, and debt data — plus assumed parameters for α and τ (tax rate). Very low priority for the scoring application; include in a research appendix if you want theoretical completeness but skip for production.

Jarrow–Turnbull Model Jarrow & Turnbull · 1995 The first reduced-form model — discrete Poisson default with recovery as a fraction of an equivalent risk-free bond.
BLOCKED⌄
P(default before T) = 1 − e^(−λT) [constant hazard] Credit spread s ≈ λ·(1 − δ) where δ = recovery rate Calibrate λ from observed spread: λ = s / (1 − δ)
What it does

Models the time-to-default τ as an exponential random variable with hazard rate λ. Default arrives as a Poisson "surprise" — there is no firm-value process to track. Upon default, the bond pays δ × (equivalent risk-free bond price), where δ is the recovery rate. λ is calibrated from observed bond yield spreads. Survival probability over horizon T: P(τ > T) = e^(−λT). The term-structure of PD follows from integrating the hazard over [0,T].

Why it works

Default in practice does feel like an exogenous surprise — it's often triggered by a covenant breach, a rating action, or a credit-line pull that causes sudden illiquidity rather than a gradual asset-value decline. The Poisson process captures this "jump-to-default" character efficiently. The model is mathematically tractable — any credit-sensitive instrument can be priced analytically as a modified risk-free bond with a credit-adjusted discount rate. Clean calibration from spread data.

Shortcomings
  • Constant hazard rate is too restrictive — credit risk is time-varying with both firm-specific and macro factors.
  • No economic content: doesn't explain why default occurs, just models when.
  • Recovery = fraction of risk-free bond is one of three competing conventions; less common in practice than Duffie-Singleton's market-value recovery.
  • λ is the risk-neutral hazard rate, not the physical probability — includes a risk premium that must be separated out for real-world PD.
  • BLOCKED: requires bond yield spreads to calibrate λ.

Duffie–Singleton Model Duffie & Singleton · 1999 · Review of Financial Studies Recovery as fraction of market value — bundles default loss into the discount rate. The industry standard for CDS desk pricing.
BLOCKED⌄
Credit-risky bond price = E^Q[exp(−∫(r_t + λ_t·L_t)dt)] where λ_t·L_t = default-adjusted spread [L = loss rate = 1 − recovery fraction] Same PDE as risk-free bond with r replaced by (r + λL)
What it does

Extends Jarrow-Turnbull with stochastic hazard rate λ(t) driven by state variables. The key innovation: recovery upon default = (1−L)·V(t^−), a fraction of the bond's market value just before default. This convention allows the credit-risky bond PDE to be written identically to the risk-free bond PDE but with a modified short rate r → r + λL — the "credit-adjusted discount rate." This makes numerical pricing methods for risk-free bonds directly applicable to credit-risky instruments.

Why it works

The market-value recovery convention is economically sensible (recovery is proportional to how much the firm is worth at default) and mathematically elegant (it collapses the two-dimensional pricing problem into one dimension). Every major bank's CDS valuation desk uses this framework. The stochastic intensity λ(t) naturally accommodates time-variation — good firms have low λ, deteriorating firms have rising λ.

Shortcomings
  • λ is risk-neutral — includes credit risk premium mixed with real-world PD. Conversion to physical PD requires specifying the market price of default risk (unobservable).
  • No structural story: doesn't explain why λ varies across firms or over time.
  • Recovery rate L must be separately estimated or assumed; conflates recovery uncertainty with PD.
  • BLOCKED: requires observable CDS or bond spreads to calibrate λ(t).

Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost / LightGBM / CatBoost) Alanis, Chava & Shah · 2022 benchmark · various implementations Sequential ensemble of shallow trees each correcting the previous — the best single model on tabular credit data in modern benchmarks.
NEEDS LABEL⌄
What it does

Builds an additive ensemble of decision trees: F(x) = Σ f_k(x). Each tree is fit to the negative gradient of the loss function computed on the residuals of all previous trees (gradient descent in function space). Trees are kept shallow (depth 3–6) to avoid overfitting; regularization is applied at both the leaf level (L1/L2) and via learning rate shrinkage. The final output is a log-odds score converted to a probability via Platt scaling or isotonic calibration. Feature importance is available via SHAP values for interpretability.

Why it works

Decision trees naturally capture nonlinear relationships and interactions — an interaction between leverage and volatility (a highly leveraged, volatile firm is much worse than either alone) is captured by a single tree split, whereas logit would require an explicit interaction term. Gradient boosting systematically reduces bias: each tree patches the regions where previous trees erred. The 2022 Alanis-Chava-Shah benchmark on 2,585 US bankruptcies (1990-2019) found excess returns, idiosyncratic volatility, and relative size are the top-3 features — all in your DB.

Shortcomings
  • Requires a large labeled dataset — small default samples produce unstable, overfit models. Your 2,724 "…Q" labels may be marginal; enrich with UCLA-LoPucki.
  • Out-of-time validation is essential — cross-validation drastically overestimates real performance on time-series data. Always hold out the last 5+ years.
  • Not inherently probabilistic — raw outputs need calibration to be PDs.
  • Class imbalance (~1% annual default rate): use scale_pos_weight or SMOTE oversampling.
  • Harder to audit for regulators vs logit — SHAP values help but don't fully substitute for coefficient interpretability.
  • Cannot run without labels — unlike Altman/Merton.

Random Forest Breiman · 2001 · various credit applications 2017–2026 Ensemble of independently-trained deep trees with random feature subsets — reduces variance by averaging uncorrelated trees.
NEEDS LABEL⌄
What it does

Trains B independent decision trees, each on a bootstrap sample of the data (bagging), and at each split considers only a random subset of √p features. Default prediction = majority vote (classification) or average of probability estimates. Unlike gradient boosting, trees are built independently (not sequentially), and each is typically grown to full depth. Feature importance via permutation importance or mean decrease in Gini impurity.

Why it works

Deep individual trees have high variance (overfit to training data) but low bias. Averaging B uncorrelated trees cancels most of the variance: Var(F̄) ≈ ρσ² + (1-ρ)σ²/B, where ρ is the inter-tree correlation. Random feature subsetting reduces ρ, making trees more independent and the average more stable. RF is particularly robust on datasets with many correlated features (like financial ratios) where feature redundancy hurts individual tree stability.

Shortcomings
  • RF vs XGBoost winner depends on dataset characteristics — XGBoost generally wins on imbalanced, noisy tabular data of this type; RF may win on smaller, cleaner samples.
  • Random forests are less efficient learners than gradient boosting: need more trees to achieve the same bias reduction.
  • Probability estimates from RF are often poorly calibrated (requires Platt scaling).
  • Slower to predict at inference time than XGBoost for large forests.

Stacked Ensemble (Model Stacking) Various · 2024–2026 literature Meta-learner combines base model outputs — often beats any individual model by exploiting uncorrelated errors.
NEEDS LABEL⌄
What it does

Two-level learning: (1) base learners — logit, RF, XGBoost, possibly Merton-DD — each produces a PD score; (2) a meta-learner (typically a small logit or ridge regression) is trained on the out-of-fold predictions of the base learners. The meta-learner learns which base models to trust in which regions of the feature space. Final prediction = meta-learner output on the base model scores.

Why it works

Each base model makes different types of errors — logit errs on nonlinear regions, RF errs on extrapolation, GBM errs on outliers. A meta-learner can learn to weight the best model contextually. The key requirement is that base models are trained with cross-validation to prevent the meta-learner from seeing in-sample predictions (which would cause leakage). When base models are diverse and their errors are uncorrelated, stacking consistently outperforms any single learner.

Shortcomings
  • More complex pipeline — requires careful cross-validation to avoid leakage.
  • Gains over the best single model are often modest (0.5–2 AUC points); the complexity cost can outweigh the benefit.
  • Harder to explain to regulators: "our PD is a logit of an XGBoost of a logit" requires careful documentation.
  • If base models are highly correlated (all learn the same thing), stacking adds no value.

Through-the-Cycle (TTC) vs Point-in-Time (PIT) Design choice — affects every model in the system Not a model — a design philosophy that determines whether your PD moves with the credit cycle or stays stable through it. Choose deliberately.
DESIGN DECISION⌄
What it does

Point-in-time (PIT): PD reflects current conditions — it rises in recessions and falls in expansions. A BBB-rated firm might have PD = 0.3% in 2019 and PD = 2.5% in 2009. CHS, Bharath-Shumway, and any model using current market prices produce PIT estimates. Through-the-cycle (TTC): PD reflects average credit quality through a full economic cycle — it is deliberately stable. A firm stays "BB" through both boom and bust unless its fundamental credit profile changes. S&P and Moody's public ratings are designed to be TTC.

Why it works

PIT is better for: risk monitoring (you want to know if Ford is more likely to default today than six months ago), equity screening, and early-warning signals. TTC is better for: credit limit setting (you don't want to cut a client's line every time the VIX spikes), capital allocation, and long-term relationship pricing. The CFA survey explicitly warns that ratings-based approaches are TTC and lag rapid deterioration — both signals are needed, but for different purposes.

Shortcomings
  • PIT: volatile — generates excessive rating migrations in transition matrices, creating large M&A/restructuring costs if used to drive credit limits directly. Noisy for long-term capital allocation.
  • TTC: lags real deterioration — CFA survey: "ratings-based approaches can miss rapid changes in fundamentals." Notoriously slow to downgrade — both 2008 and 2022 saw major rating actions after market prices had already moved.
  • The hybrid approach: most sophisticated systems run PIT models for daily monitoring and compute a TTC grade by averaging PIT PD across a trailing full credit cycle (5–7 years).

What it does

Simple, transparent, requires no model specification beyond the rating assignment step. Easy to explain to regulators, credit committees, and non-quant stakeholders.   • Low-default grades have very few observed defaults → high estimation error for investment-grade buckets.

Duration Analysis (Survival Analysis) Cox · 1972 · applied to credit by Shumway 2001, Chava-Jarrow 2004 Continuous-time survival model using all firm-observations including right-censored survivors — more efficient than cohort analysis.
NEEDS LABEL⌄
What it does

Models time-to-default T_i for each firm using a hazard function h(t|X_i) = h_0(t)·exp(β·X_i(t)). The Cox proportional hazard model doesn't require specifying the baseline hazard h_0(t) — it's estimated semi-parametrically. Each firm contributes information up to its event time (default) or censoring time (survived to end of sample, merged, etc.). Partial likelihood estimation is efficient and handles right-censoring correctly. Output: hazard rates and survival probabilities for any time horizon, by covariate profile.

Why it works

Using all firm-quarters (not just a snapshot) maximizes statistical efficiency. Censored observations contribute their full survival history — a firm that survived 20 years without defaulting provides strong evidence that its profile is low-risk, not "missing data." The proportional hazard form lets you estimate relative risk (e.g., "each unit increase in TLMTA multiplies the default rate by e^β") while remaining agnostic about the absolute level of the baseline hazard.

Shortcomings
  • Proportionality assumption: β is assumed constant over time (covariates have the same multiplicative effect on hazard at all durations). Violated if, say, leverage matters more for short-term than long-term default — test with Schoenfeld residuals.
  • Assumes non-informative censoring: firms that merge or delist for non-credit reasons are assumed to have the same default probability as firms that survive. M&A censoring is likely informative (acquirees tend to be healthier).
  • Time-varying covariates require a "counting process" extension (Andersen-Gill formulation).

Rating Transition Matrices S&P, Moody's methodology · CECL / IFRS 9 standard tool N×N matrix of rating migration probabilities — converts 1-year PD to a full lifetime PD term structure for any horizon.
NEEDS LABEL + RATINGS⌄
What it does

An N×N matrix M where M[i,j] = probability that a firm in rating grade i at time t migrates to grade j by time t+1. The last column (grade D = default) is the 1-year PD by grade. Because default is an absorbing state, M[D,D] = 1. Power the matrix to get multi-year transitions: M^t gives the t-year migration probabilities. Extract the last column of M^t → PD at year t for each starting grade. This produces the lifetime PD curve needed for CECL/IFRS 9.

Why it works

Captures both default risk AND migration risk (a BBB firm isn't just at risk of defaulting; it's at risk of being downgraded to BB, which increases future borrowing costs and limits access to capital). The matrix approach is the natural way to compute multi-period probabilities consistent with single-period transitions — matrix powers are the mathematical machinery. CECL requires lifetime expected loss, which is the integral of conditional PD at each future period, discounted — transition matrices produce exactly this.

Shortcomings
  • Markov assumption: next rating depends only on current rating, not rating history. Empirically violated — firms recently downgraded have higher downgrade probability than firms that have held the same grade for years ("momentum" or "rating drift" effect).
  • TTC vs PIT: empirical transition matrices are usually TTC; during recessions, all migrations worsen more than the historical average predicts. Apply macro conditioning for stress scenarios.
  • Estimation error is high for speculative grades and long horizons — low default counts make M^5 or M^10 unreliable without MPEP floors.

Most Prudent Estimation Principle (MPEP) Basel III / EBA guidelines Statistical floor for low-default portfolios — prevents PD estimates of zero when observed defaults are rare but positive risk clearly exists.
APPLY FORMULAICALLY⌄
What it does

When empirical default rates are zero or very low for a rating grade (e.g., no AAA-rated firms defaulted in your sample), the maximum likelihood estimate gives PD = 0. MPEP replaces this with the upper bound of a one-sided confidence interval: PD_MPEP = 1 − (1−α)^(1/n) ≈ α/n for confidence level α and cohort size n. For example: 0 defaults in a cohort of 100 at 90% confidence → PD_MPEP = 1 − (0.10)^(0.01) ≈ 2.3%. Basel III requires this floor for IRB banks.

Why it works

A PD estimate of exactly zero is epistemically overconfident — it implies the future can never produce a default in that grade, which contradicts all experience. The MPEP quantifies the statistical uncertainty in the estimate: with a finite sample, zero observed defaults doesn't mean zero probability of default. The one-sided CI gives the highest PD consistent with the observed data at a given confidence level, providing a conservative but statistically justified floor.

Shortcomings
  • Somewhat arbitrary choice of α (typically 90% or 95%) creates systematically inflated PDs for the highest-quality grades.
  • Produces discontinuous PD estimates across grades when cohort sizes vary (larger cohorts → smaller MPEP adjustment).
  • Only relevant for very thin rating buckets with near-zero observed defaults — won't affect speculative-grade estimates materially.
Your data

Shows that in a single-factor credit model, both PD and recovery rate R are driven by the same latent systematic factor Z (the state of the economy). When Z is low (recession), defaults rise AND recoveries fall simultaneously. Frye derives a closed-form expression for the joint distribution. The "downturn LGD" required by Basel III is the conditional expected LGD given a stress scenario — e.g., Z at the 2.5th percentile of its distribution.

Why it works

In bad economic times, fire sales (Acharya et al.), illiquid secondary markets, and industry-wide distress all worsen simultaneously — so recovery rates collapse precisely when default rates peak. Ignoring this co-movement gives a systematically optimistic expected loss: EL_stressed ≠ PD_stressed × LGD_average; you need PD_stressed × LGD_stressed. The 2008–09 crisis validated this sharply: default rates surged to ~12% while recovery rates on HY bonds fell to ~25–30% vs the long-run ~40%.

Shortcomings
  • Fitting ρ_R precisely requires paired PD and recovery observations — which you don't have. Must use published estimates (ρ ≈ 0.3–0.5 for US corporates).
  • Single-factor model is a simplification; different industries may have different systematic exposures.
  • The static mean R_mean still needs calibration from recovery data or a published study.
Your data

Use FRED NFCI or BAMLH0A0HYM2 (HY OAS) as Z. Apply the formula with published ρ ≈ 0.4 and R_mean from Altman-Kishore/Moody's tables. Compute downturn LGD as the Frye-adjusted LGD at Z = 1.96 standard-deviation stress — this is the Basel III "downturn LGD" approximation. Acharya–Bharath–Srinivasan: Industry Distress & Fire Sales Acharya, Bharath & Srinivasan · 2007 · Journal of Finance Recovery is materially lower when the defaulting firm's industry is itself in distress — potential asset buyers are also impaired.
PARTIAL — sector proxy available⌄
What it does

Documents that recovery rates at default are significantly lower — by 10–20 percentage points — when the defaulting firm's industry is simultaneously experiencing distress (low industry stock returns, high industry default rates). The natural buyers in a bankruptcy auction (industry peers who can optimally deploy the assets) are themselves distressed and unable to bid, leaving only outsiders who value the assets less. This "fire-sale discount" is systematic, not random.

Why it works

Asset specificity is the core mechanism: steel mills are most valuable to other steelmakers, retail real estate to other retailers, oil rigs to other E&P companies. When the whole sector is distressed simultaneously, the natural buyers can't finance acquisitions — they're managing their own balance sheets. Outside buyers (private equity, distressed funds) demand a large discount for assets they can't optimally operate. The fire-sale discount grows with asset specificity and industry leverage.

Shortcomings
  • Hard to forecast ex ante: you need to predict whether the firm's sector will be distressed at the time of default, which is uncertain at origination.
  • The adjustment is an ex-post observation, not a forward-looking model — converts to a scenario adjustment rather than a precise LGD estimate.
  • Industry asset specificity varies — the fire-sale effect is strong for capital-intensive industries (energy, materials) and weak for asset-light sectors (software, services).

What it does

LGD = 1 − (PV of all recoveries from a defaulted exposure) / EAD, where recoveries are discounted at the bank's cost of capital over the full workout period (months to years). Why it works

Workout LGD is the economically correct definition: it counts every dollar actually recovered (including proceeds from collateral liquidation, debt restructuring, or asset sales) discounted for the time value of money and workout cost. Market LGD (bond price 30 days post-default) is noisier and may mis-estimate recovery when post-default bond prices are distressed. The fractional response model handles the [0,1] support without the problems of truncated regression or log-transform artifacts at the boundaries.

What it does

Vector autoregression (VAR) models the joint dynamics of K macro variables as a system: each variable is regressed on lags of itself and all others. Apply a macro shock (e.g., GDP −3%, unemployment +4pp, HY OAS +400bps) and simulate forward — the VAR generates internally consistent paths for all variables simultaneously. Feed these macro paths into your firm-level PD model as time-varying covariates to get a stressed portfolio default rate distribution at each future period.

Why it works

Individual firm-level stress tests (shocking each firm's ratios independently) ignore the systemic feedback: rising defaults → tighter credit conditions → higher defaults (the financial accelerator). VAR captures these feedback loops by modeling all macro variables jointly. The scenario consistency is also critical: you can't stress HY OAS without also worsening GDP and unemployment — a VAR ensures the stressed path is internally coherent. This is the methodology underlying the Fed's DFAST bank stress tests.

Shortcomings
  • VAR has K² × lag parameters — with 6 variables and 4 lags, that's 144 parameters to estimate. Prone to overfitting on short macro time series.
  • Linear dynamics miss threshold effects — financial crises involve nonlinear feedback (sudden liquidity dry-up, contagion) that VAR systematically underestimates.
  • Historical data contains very few severe stress episodes (2-3 recessions with different characters) — tail calibration is unreliable.
  • Not a firm-level model: it estimates aggregate/portfolio default rates, not individual PDs. Must combine with firm-level models via a two-stage approach.

Equity–bond pricing disconnects occur when equity markets and bond/CDS markets assign materially different implied credit quality to the same firm. Understanding why they happen, which direction they resolve, and how to exploit or account for them is essential for any hybrid credit model. This tab covers the theory, documented empirical patterns, the CDS-bond basis, data sources for fixed-income, and design implications for your pipeline.

1 · Why theory says they shouldn't diverge

Under the Merton (1974) framework, equity and credit risk are two faces of the same underlying asset process. Equity = call option on firm assets; debt = risk-free bond − put option on firm assets. Both are driven solely by:

  • Asset value V — as V falls, equity falls and credit spreads rise simultaneously
  • Asset volatility σ_V — higher vol raises both equity implied vol (via BSM) and PD (via the DD formula)
  • Leverage F/V — more debt raises both the option's moneyness for equity and the probability of breaching the default barrier

The model predicts a deterministic, monotone relationship between equity volatility and credit spreads for any given level of leverage. Equity option markets and CDS markets should agree on PD by construction.

2 · Why they diverge in practice

Different Investor Bases & Market Microstructure Bond buyers and equity traders have different mandates, time horizons, and information sets — prices don't have to agree.
⌄
The structural gap

Investment-grade bond buyers are predominantly insurance companies, pension funds, and bond mutual funds — they are buy-and-hold, yield-focused, and supply-constrained. They respond slowly to new information because selling a bond requires finding a counterparty in an OTC market. Equity traders are short-term, momentum-driven, and transact in centralized, high-liquidity markets. The two markets genuinely receive and incorporate information at different speeds.

Who leads whom

In deterioration: equity markets typically price distress first — equity is more liquid, shorter settlement, and equity options let traders take quick levered positions on vol. Blanco, Brennan & Marsh (2005) showed CDS spreads lead bond spreads in price discovery, but equity leads CDS by hours to days for fast-moving events. In improvement: distressed debt investors (who know the restructuring plan) often lead equity — the "debt-to-equity" trade works when bond buyers know more about recovery value than public equity markets.

Documented empirical gap

Collin-Dufresne, Goldstein & Martin (2001) showed that changes in equity returns, leverage, and volatility explain only 25% of the variation in credit spread changes — far less than Merton predicts. The remainder is explained by a single common factor that looks like a supply/demand or liquidity factor in the bond market, not firm-specific fundamentals. This is a serious challenge to pure structural models.

Implication for your model

Your naïve-DD and CHS scores are equity-derived. They will miss disconnects where bond markets have already priced stress that equity hasn't (the bond market "knows first" in restructuring situations). Monitoring the Damodaran synthetic-rating spread vs the equity-implied PD gives a rough cross-check without needing live bond data.

The Zombie Firm Phenomenon Equity has option value even when bonds trade near default — shareholders rationally hold a deeply out-of-the-money call.
⌄
What happens

When a firm is deeply distressed (bonds at 40 cents on the dollar), equity should theoretically trade near zero. But equity often maintains a non-trivial market cap — sometimes 10–30% of pre-distress levels. Example: Hertz equity continued trading at $1–2 even after the company filed Chapter 11 in May 2020, and retail investors actually drove it higher. The bond market correctly priced near-total loss while the equity market embedded option value and lottery-ticket demand.

Why equity retains value

Under Black-Scholes, a deeply out-of-the-money call is worth more than zero as long as there is nonzero probability the firm's assets recover above the debt face value before maturity. In practice: (1) Chapter 11 allows the firm to continue operating — there's a real chance of reorganization with equity recovery; (2) retail speculators treat deeply distressed equity as lottery tickets; (3) short-selling constraints prevent arbitrageurs from driving equity to zero. The bond market correctly values the debt claim; equity is pricing optionality and vol.

Modeling implication

Structural models (Merton, naïve-DD) that read equity price as the primary input will systematically understate PD for zombie firms — equity vol rises (good signal) but the market-cap-to-debt ratio doesn't collapse as much as it should (bad signal distortion). The naïve-DD denominator (σ_V) correctly captures the rising vol signal; the numerator (ln((E+F)/F)) is contaminated by inflated E. This is one reason the KMV empirical mapping outperforms N(−DD): it's calibrated on actual observed defaults, not the theoretical normal distribution.

Detection in your data

Flag potential zombies: firms with vol_90d > 0.80 (annualized) AND naïve-DD < 0.5 AND net_debt_to_mcap > 5 — these are high-vol, near-default names where equity still has material market cap. Cross-check with interest_coverage < 1 (can't cover interest from operating income). This cohort warrants manual review.

Capital Structure Arbitrage Trading equity vs CDS to exploit the gap between equity-implied PD and market-implied PD — the practitioner application of the Merton model.
⌄
The trade

If the equity-implied PD (from Merton/naïve-DD) is much lower than the CDS-implied PD (from the spread), the bond market is pricing more credit risk than the equity market. A capital structure arbitrageur buys CDS protection (shorting credit) while going long equity — betting that either (a) equity reprices down to reflect the bond market's view, or (b) CDS spreads tighten to reflect the equity market's optimism. The reverse trade applies when equity implies more stress than bonds. Yu (2006) documented that convergence happened ~60% of the time, but losses during non-convergence were severe.

Why it persists

The arb should eliminate itself quickly in efficient markets. It persists because: (1) neither equity vol nor CDS spreads are perfectly observed or synchronized; (2) the Merton mapping requires assumptions (debt maturity T, default barrier) that introduce noise; (3) the trade requires holding both a bond/CDS position and an equity position — different balance sheets, counterparties, and margin requirements create practical frictions; (4) the disconnect can widen before converging (basis risk), triggering margin calls that force liquidation before convergence.

Risks & failures

The CDS-equity arb blew up spectacularly for several hedge funds in 2005 (GM and Ford downgrade to junk) and again in 2008. The core failure: both equity and CDS moved adversely simultaneously (GM equity crashed AND CDS widened further) because a systematic credit event overwhelmed the theoretical relationship. The trade assumes mean-reversion of the spread between equity-implied and CDS-implied PD; in a regime shift, both re-anchor to new levels without converging to each other.

For your pipeline

Without CDS data, you can't run the full arb. But computing the gap between your equity-derived PD (naïve-DD) and your accounting-derived PD (CHS, O-score) flags names where the two signals diverge — a weaker but data-available version of the same signal. Large positive gap (equity says safe, accounting says distress) is a yellow flag worth investigating. This is implementable today with your DB.

The CDS–Bond Basis CDS spread and bond spread should be equal by no-arbitrage. When they diverge, it reveals market stress, funding constraints, and technical dislocations.
⌄
The theoretical relationship

By no-arbitrage, buying a corporate bond and buying CDS protection on the same name should replicate a risk-free bond. Therefore: CDS spread ≈ bond spread over LIBOR/SOFR (the "CDS-bond basis" = CDS spread − bond spread should ≈ 0). If the basis is positive (CDS > bond spread), CDS market prices more default risk. If negative (CDS < bond spread), the bond market is cheaper — driven by supply/demand technicals, illiquidity, or repo market constraints.

What drives the basis negative (bonds cheap)

In 2008, the basis went deeply negative (–200 to –500 bps for many names). Dealers who would normally arbitrage it were capital-constrained and couldn't fund bond purchases on repo. Institutional investors were forced sellers regardless of price. This "negative basis" trade became famous — buy the cheap bond, buy CDS protection — but required funding that was unavailable precisely when the opportunity was largest. Bao, Pan & Wang (2011) showed the negative basis is highly correlated with bond market illiquidity (bid-ask spreads) and dealer inventory constraints.

What drives the basis positive (CDS expensive)

Positive basis occurs when: (1) CDS demand from hedgers exceeds supply from protection sellers — common when credit deteriorates and everyone wants to buy protection simultaneously; (2) "cheapest to deliver" optionality in CDS (protection buyers can deliver the cheapest eligible bond upon default, which is worth more than a specific bond — CDS should be slightly more expensive); (3) regulatory changes that restrict bank CDS writing. The basis turned sharply positive for many European banks in 2011–12.

For your pipeline

Without CDS or bond data, you can't compute the basis. However, FRED's HY and IG OAS series (BAMLH0A0HYM2, BAMLC0A0CM) are computed from actual bond prices and effectively give you the aggregate bond-spread side of the basis for the index. If you eventually add TRACE data (below), you can compute name-level bond spreads and compare against your equity-derived PD — giving you an approximate basis signal for each name with bonds outstanding.

Non-Default Components of Credit Spreads Up to 50% of IG credit spreads may reflect liquidity risk, tax effects, and risk premiums — not default probability at all.
⌄
The decomposition

Longstaff, Mithal & Neis (2005) used CDS as a "pure credit" benchmark to decompose corporate bond spreads into: (1) default component — the CDS spread for the same name/maturity; (2) non-default component — the residual. They found the non-default component was large and time-varying, averaging 50–100 bps for IG names. It was driven by bond market illiquidity, individual investor tax effects (income vs capital gains), and supply/demand technicals specific to the bond market.

Why this matters for your model

If you calibrate a reduced-form hazard rate from bond spreads (Jarrow-Turnbull / Duffie-Singleton), you will overstate PD — you're capturing the full spread including the non-default component. This is one reason CDS is preferred for credit model calibration: it's a cleaner signal of default risk. It also means that a firm's bond spread can widen without its actual default probability rising — driven purely by bond-market illiquidity or forced selling. Your equity-derived models (naïve-DD, CHS) are immune to this bond-market noise, which is actually an advantage.

Implications for stress testing

In crises, bond spreads widen far more than default risk alone justifies — the liquidity component can add 200–400 bps for IG names and 500–1000 bps for HY in a panic. Using raw bond spreads as stress inputs (e.g., "spread is 800 bps → default soon") systematically overstates PD during liquidity crises. Conversely, using equity-derived PD during the same crisis may understate it (zombie-firm problem). The right answer is a blend — which is exactly what your architecture provides.

In your pipeline

When you pull FRED HY OAS as a macro covariate, be aware it combines default risk + liquidity premium. Use it as a credit-cycle barometer, not as a direct PD input. The NFCI has a dedicated "credit" sub-index that partially decomposes the two — prefer that for systematic risk calibration. This is the key reason to run both equity-based and accounting-based models in parallel: their errors are uncorrelated in different parts of the crisis cycle.

What you get

FINRA's Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine (TRACE) captures every OTC corporate bond transaction (trade price, size, direction) with a 4-hour delay for public dissemination. For investment-grade bonds, you can reconstruct daily yield curves per issuer, compute OAS vs the Treasury curve, and derive name-level credit spreads — for free. Covers investment-grade, high-yield, agency, and ABS. Historical data goes back to 2002 (2005 for IG). Access via finra.org/finra-data/fixed-income or the FINRA Data API.

The academic / research path

WRDS (Wharton Research Data Services) provides cleaned, enhanced TRACE data (TRACE Enhanced) with correction for known data errors (Dick-Nielsen 2009 cleaning algorithm is standard). Academic institutions typically have WRDS access. If you can access WRDS, TRACE Enhanced + Mergent FISD (bond characteristics: coupon, maturity, seniority, covenants) gives you a full bond-level dataset comparable to Bloomberg for credit research. WRDS subscription: ~$10–30K/yr institutional.

Limitations
  • 4-hour delay — not suitable for real-time trading signals, fine for daily credit monitoring.
  • Many small-cap companies have no publicly traded bonds — TRACE won't have spread data for ~60% of your Sharadar universe.
  • Computing clean OAS from raw TRACE trades requires duration matching to the Treasury curve and bond characteristic data (maturity, coupon) — meaningful data engineering effort.
  • Coverage improves dramatically for market-cap > $5B — your largest names will have good TRACE coverage.
For your pipeline

Pull TRACE for your largest 200–300 names (where bond coverage is reliable). Compute a simple yield spread over the matched-maturity Treasury curve. 4 · Practical disconnect monitor: what you can build today

Without live CDS or bond data, a useful equity-bond disconnect signal can be constructed entirely from your existing DB:

1
Model disagreement score: Compute both naïve-DD PD (equity-derived) and CHS/O-score PD (accounting-derived) for every name. Large positive gap = equity says safe but fundamentals say distress. Large negative gap = equity has priced in distress faster than the accounting model. The gap itself is a signal — names where both models agree have higher-confidence PD estimates.
2
Synthetic spread vs equity PD: Compute Damodaran synthetic-rating spread from interest_coverage. Convert to an implied PD. Compare against naïve-DD PD. The coverage-implied spread is an accounting analog to a bond spread — when it diverges significantly from the equity-derived PD, flag the name for review.
3
TRACE overlay for large names: Pull TRACE data for your top 200–300 by market cap. Compute the yield spread vs the matched Treasury. Plot against naïve-DD PD for those names. This is your best free equity-bond disconnect monitor and gives you real bond market data for the names where it matters most.
4
FRED aggregate regime: When HY OAS exceeds 700 bps (deep stress regime) AND your equity-derived PD model shows less than proportional increase, the bond market is likely pricing illiquidity premium on top of default risk. Apply a model-specific stress scalar (e.g., multiply all PDs by 1.3×) to account for the systematic component that pure equity models miss in crises.

# Bharath-Shumway (2008) naive DD — validated against backtester.duckdb
# vol_90d is already ANNUALIZED in sharadar_prices_computed — do NOT multiply by sqrt(252)
import math

def naive_dd(E, F, sig_E, r_1y):
    """E=marketcap_ffill, F=debtc+0.5*debtnc, sig_E=vol_90d, r_1y=mom_12m1m"""
    if E <= 0 or F <= 0 or sig_E <= 0:
        return float('nan'), float('nan')
    sig_V = (E/(E+F))*sig_E + (F/(E+F))*(0.05 + 0.25*sig_E)
    DD    = (math.log((E+F)/F) + (r_1y - 0.5*sig_V**2)) / sig_V
    PD    = 0.5*(1 + math.erf(-DD/math.sqrt(2)))   # N(-DD)
    return DD, PD
