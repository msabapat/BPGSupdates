1 · Idio Vol vs. Beta Vol vs. Total Vol

Which volatility component best predicts what?Mixed

Every stock's realized vol can be split into a systematic (market+group beta-driven) piece and an idiosyncratic (residual) piece, using the locked 12-month half-life blend across the 1m/3m/6m/12m beta regressions. The question: does the split add predictive power over just using plain trailing volatility?

Forward volatility: all three classifiers predict next-month realized vol about equally well — total vol Q4−Q1 spread +22.2pp/yr (t=27.95), idio vol +19.3pp (t=27.35), beta vol +19.0pp (t=23.89). The split doesn't sharpen vol forecasting; plain total vol is just as good.

Forward alpha (idiosyncratic return): here idio vol looked like a much stronger signal at first — a −9.6%/yr Q4−Q1 spread (t=−5.76) vs. beta vol's −4.3%/yr (t=−3.15), roughly 2× stronger and consistent with the well-known idio-vol puzzle (Ang-Hodrick-Xing-Zhang). A follow-up 4×4 past-beta × past-alpha grid was built specifically to stress-test this, and the result reversed the conclusion: the negative-alpha spread is largely a mechanical artifact of stale trailing-intercept bias. No combination of past beta/past alpha (market or industry-group beta, both tested) produces a statistically significant forward total-return spread (all t≤1.4) — the beta premium roughly cancels the residual-alpha drift once measured correctly.

Verdict: the beta/idio-vol decomposition remains genuinely useful for risk classification (it's the backbone of the MV-frontier and beta-bucket work below), but it is not an exploitable standalone return signal. This closes that specific research thread.

3 · Industry Group Signals

Do Morningstar industry groups show predictive momentum, beta, or vol effects?Weak / mixed

~74–94 Morningstar industry groups within the top-500 universe, tested for whether trailing group-level return, beta, or (newly computed) realized volatility predict forward group performance.

Momentum: weak and not statistically significant — Fama-MacBeth correlations of trailing vs. next-month CAGR are only +0.02 to +0.03 (t=1.2–1.7). Beta: a mild betting-against-beta effect (low-beta industries outperform slightly), strengthening at longer measurement windows. Short-horizon (1–3 month) signals clearly beat 6–12 month signals: a top-1-month-total-return industry pick produced 13.3% CAGR at −51% max DD, vs. 6–12m signals trailing SPY.

Volatility (new): no industry-level volatility existed in the database — it was computed properly as an equal-weighted daily industry basket → rolling 63-day realized vol, rather than averaging individual stock vols (which understates true basket vol by ignoring correlation structure). Bottom-quartile-vol industries combined with a top-1-month-return tilt gave the best risk-adjusted line of the whole thread: 9.8–10.1% CAGR at SPY-like volatility with only −36% to −39% max drawdown (vs. SPY's −50.8%).

Caveat: single/paired-industry concentration bets are risky on their own (−68% to −84% drawdowns at 6–12m horizons) — the value of this research is less as a standalone strategy and more as a filter layered onto the existing strategies (next section).

4 · Applying Industry-Vol Filters to MOM / LV / MR

A real improvement to the locked MOM strategyWin

Classified each locked strategy's actual holdings by the industry-vol quartile of the industry group they belong to, to see whether excluding high-industry-vol names improves risk-adjusted performance.

LV already concentrates in low-industry-vol groups (80% in Q1) by construction — a filter adds nothing new. MOM's Sharpe decays monotonically with industry vol (0.97 in Q1 → 0.23 in Q4) — a strong, exploitable pattern. MR mirror-images MOM, doing best in the highest-industry-vol quartile (0.40 Sharpe) — consistent with mean-reversion feeding on volatility rather than avoiding it.

Confirms the mean-reversion mechanismConfirms hypothesis

Decomposed MR's realized return by whether the volatility driving each holding was idiosyncratic (stock-specific) or industry-driven, to test the hypothesis that MR's edge comes from stock-specific overreaction rather than industry-wide moves.

Idiosyncratic-driven volatility is monotonically good for MR (0.24 → 0.69 Sharpe); industry-driven volatility is monotonically bad (0.83 → 0.42) — exactly what the mean-reversion thesis predicts: MR wants stock-specific overreactions to fade, not industry-wide selloffs that may keep going.

Direct test — idio-weighted Stage 2 selector. Took the finding literally and rebuilt MR's Stage 2 ranking metric as composite = var_pct_idio(3m) × signal_70d_return instead of raw signal_70d_return — still picking the most-negative names, but a large industry-driven drop with low idio share now gets down-weighted relative to a smaller, more idiosyncratic one. Stage 1 (FCF-yield top-150 quality pool) and everything else stayed at the locked config (N2000, $20M liquidity, net_debt≤1, equal weight, buf=2, daily signal/trade). Result: the idio-weighted selector does not beat the base signal. It underperforms in-sample at both X=10 and X=20 (0.65 vs. 0.76, 0.59 vs. 0.73), and out-of-sample it's clearly worse at X=10 (0.25 vs. 0.53). The one bright spot is X=20 OOS, where idio-weighting edges out the base (0.54 vs. 0.44) with a shallower drawdown (−22.2% vs. −25.6%) — but that's one cell out of four, working against a consistent IS pattern of underperformance, so it reads as noise rather than a confirmed improvement. Conclusion: the vol-share decomposition explains MR's return well diagnostically, but turning it directly into the Stage-2 ranking metric does not improve the strategy — the raw oversold signal, filtered through the FCF-quality Stage-1 gate, remains the better selector. Not adopted.

6 · A Beta-Bucket Substitute for LV

Looked great in-sample, collapsed out-of-sampleRejected

Tested whether the market-β × industry-group-β 4×4 bucket grid, combined with a fundamental quality screen, could produce a genuinely non-overlapping, lower-volatility substitute for the existing LV strategy.

Root cause: low market-beta is not the same thing as low total volatility. Beta-sorted names carried much higher idiosyncratic vol out-of-sample (23.6% avg vol_60d) than LV's directly-vol-selected book (16.7%) — the beta-bucket approach was implicitly picking up idiosyncratic risk that a pure total-vol sort screens out. Also found: market-beta is a far stronger risk-sorter than group-beta (main effect on Sortino ≈0.75 vs. ≈0.21), and FCF-yield screening does virtually all the useful quality-screening work in this framework (net-debt cap turned out to be non-binding).

Verdict: no viable non-overlapping LV substitute found this round. Flagged next step (not yet built): gate on idiosyncratic vol directly (from the decomposition's residual variance) rather than market-beta, paired with the FCF-yield ranking, and run through the real trading engine with weekly signal + hysteresis rather than a monthly snapshot.

7 · Where This Leaves Us
Confirmed useful: beta/idio-vol decomposition for risk classification (MV frontier, bucket work); industry-vol as a filter on MOM (real IS+OOS Sharpe and drawdown improvement); the idiosyncratic-vs-industry vol split as a lens on MR's mean-reversion mechanism.
Ruled out: beta/idio-vol decomposition as a standalone alpha signal (mechanical artifact); market-beta buckets as a standalone LV substitute (fails OOS on the beta≠vol distinction); idio-vol-weighted MR Stage-2 selector (underperforms the base signal at X=10 both IS and OOS, and IS at X=20 — the one OOS win at X=20 reads as noise against that pattern).
