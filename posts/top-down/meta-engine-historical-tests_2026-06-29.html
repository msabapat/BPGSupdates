Building a Regime Meta-Engine

What seven historical tests teach about deciding regimes with only the information available at the time

BPGS · 2026-06-25

1. Purpose

We want a system that changes which strategies it builds and runs as the world changes, instead of a fixed set of strategies. Before building it, we tested the idea on seven past episodes — including the two most severe crises of the last 30 years. For each, we asked: using only what was knowable at the time, could a disciplined process have made the right call? Mostly yes — but the useful part is how, where it falls short, and why a purely statistical version does not work.

A note on “right” and “wrong” — read first. Every call in this document is labelled with hindsight. At the time, the wrong branch was genuinely defensible and widely held by smart people; we know which one was correct only because the outcome is now history. The point is not to claim these calls were obvious — they were not. The point is to ask a narrower question: was there a specific, measurable variable, visible on the day, that separated the two branches? If yes, the interesting test is whether that kind of variable generalises to the next episode. Labelling the past is easy; the value is in the discriminator, not the verdict.

2. Why a fixed quant warning signal is backward-looking

The natural first instinct is a single composite “crash indicator.” We built one as honestly as possible. Its components were chosen only from what had measurably broken in crises before 2003 — an inverting yield curve (1990, 2000), widening credit spreads (1990, 1998), a volatility spike (1987, 1998), and a break in price trend (1987, 2000). Each is standardized against its own trailing year, the four are averaged into one score, and an alarm is raised above 1.5 standard deviations. The rule was then run unchanged from 2003 to 2024 — out of sample for every crisis that followed. We did not let the choice of components look ahead, and we left valuation out because deep daily valuation data is not readily available (and none of the test crises was primarily a valuation bubble).

It did not work, and the way it failed is the point:

  • It cried wolf — 22 separate alarms for 3 actual crises, about seven false alarms per real one.
  • It confirmed more than it warned (composite score, before vs after the market peak):

The reason is structural. A composite can only contain signals that flagged past crises, so it is tuned to crisis types already seen. The few signals reliable enough to include across all of them — volatility, trend, drawdown — only move once the decline is underway, so they confirm rather than predict. A fitted indicator is backward-looking by construction: it recognizes the last crisis, not the next. The rest of this document therefore works in terms of causes and discriminators, not a fitted score.

3. Method — what we are actually building, and how

For each episode we build one thing: a single, explicit decision rule that a machine could have run on the day, and then we grade it against history without letting the answer leak in. Concretely, four steps:

1. Fix the information set. Take only the data an investor genuinely had on the decision date — interest rates, credit spreads, the yield curve, prices — and use series that are never revised. A Treasury yield or a stock price printed in 2007 still reads the same today, so we cannot accidentally feed in a number that only existed later. (Revised series like GDP are avoided for exactly this reason.)

2. State one rule, as cause and effect. For example: “when the real interest rate is falling, long-duration assets such as gold and long bonds rise.” The rule names a mechanism and a measurable trigger, and it never mentions the outcome. The identical rule is applied to every date, including the dates where it loses money — no quietly skipping the bad years.

3. Write down the opposing trade. For each episode we record the reasonable-but-wrong call that many people actually made at the time (e.g. “money-printing means inflation — sell bonds”), and we name the one variable that separated it from the right call. If no such variable was visible on the day, the episode was a genuine coin-toss and we say so rather than pretending the rule would have helped.

4. Score against the outcome, separately and last. Only after the rule and the two branches are fixed do we look at what happened, using total-return prices. Our knowledge of how things turned out is allowed into the grading, never into the decision.

That separation is the whole discipline. It is what lets us say something honest about whether a rule had real foresight, instead of quietly fitting the past.

4. The common framework — and its honest limit

Across all seven episodes the same shape kept appearing. The big fact everyone could see — rates at zero, rates rising, a crash in progress — was never the edge, because everyone could see it and it was already in prices. The edge was always a second, less-watched variable that decided which way the obvious fact would resolve. Think of it as a poker table: every player sees the same cards on the board; the skill is the one extra read that tells you whether the obvious story is the true one.

So each episode reduces to three parts:

  1. Observation — the obvious fact (cheap to see, already priced).
  2. Two branches — the same fact supports a bullish and a bearish reading, both defensible.
  3. Discriminator — the second variable that tips it one way. This is the only part that carries information, and finding it is the entire job.

Each discriminator turns out to be one of two kinds, and intuitively they behave very differently:

Coincident — the discriminator is itself a traded price (the real interest rate, a credit spread, the volatility index). Because it is a price, it cannot warn you in advance: if it could be predicted, it would already have moved. It is like a thermometer reading — it tells you the temperature now, exactly when the assets it judges are moving. So you use it as a trigger: act as it happens, size fully, and expect it to expire just as abruptly.

Leading — the discriminator is a slow structural condition (the shape of the yield curve, the level of valuation). It is like a fever building before the illness shows: it tells you trouble is coming but not when, often a year early. So you use it as a bias: tilt gradually, size partially, and expect to be early. Used as a trigger it loses money by acting too soon. Simple test to classify a new signal: is it a price you could trade today (coincident), or a slow condition (leading)?

The honest limit. We can only sort the branches into “right” and “wrong” because we now know the endings. At the time the two branches looked comparably sensible, and capable people took the side that lost. What the framework can fairly claim is narrower but still useful: in six of the seven episodes a concrete discriminator, visible on the day, did separate the branches — and the kind of discriminator (price vs structural state) reliably told you how to use it. Whether this carries to the next, unseen crisis is the open question the whole meta-engine is built to face — not a thing these backward-looking labels can prove.

5. The historical tests

5.1  Zero rates, 2009–2011

Observation: The Fed cut to zero and real rates were negative and falling.

The two branches: Right — falling real rates lift long-duration and zero-yield assets (gold, long bonds) and push money into credit. Wrong, and popular — money printing causes near-term inflation; sell bonds, buy commodities, expect a dollar collapse.

Discriminator available then: Bank credit was contracting (−19% year-on-year in 2009), core inflation low and falling (0.7% in 2010), bond-market inflation expectations near 2%. All said inflation was not coming.

Type: Coincident. Monthly changes in real rates lined up with gold and long-bond returns at zero lag (about −0.43 and −0.39).

Outcome: Gold roughly doubled 2009–2012; long bonds +34% in 2011; the inflation trade lost. When real rates turned up in 2013, gold fell 28% that same year.

Lesson: A coincident signal can be acted on as it happens — and it expires as it happens. The 2013 turn was the exit, in real time.

5.2  Rising rates, 2017–2018

Observation: The Fed was raising short rates and shrinking its balance sheet.

The two branches: Naive — rates up, buy banks and value. Right — rising short rates with a flattening curve signal late cycle; favor defensives and quality; banks lag despite higher rates.

Discriminator available then: The shape of the yield curve — 10-year minus 2-year fell from 123 bp at the start of 2017 to 21 bp by the end of 2018, visible daily.

Outcome: Utilities +3.9% in 2018; S&P 500 −4.6%; banks −13% despite four rate hikes. Defensives outperformed throughout, exactly as the flattening curve implied.

Type: Leading. The flattening was clear a full year before the late-2018 outcome.

Lesson: A leading signal tells you the state correctly but early. It gave the “what” a year ahead and nothing about the “when.” As a trigger it would have cost the 2017 rally.

5.3  Tech peak, 2021

Observation: Technology led the market; the 1990s comparison was everywhere.

The two branches: Naive — buy the leaders. Right — the 1990s comparison points to 1999–2000, a top, and the other evidence (extreme valuation, rising inflation, a Fed about to tighten) said late-stage.

Discriminator available then: The valuation level (near its 2000 high) with the inflation and rate trajectory.

Outcome: Nasdaq-100 fell 33% in 2022 as real rates surged to +2%. It then recovered through 2023–24 driven by AI earnings — not rate cuts — leaving a buy-and-hold position roughly flat over four years after a severe drawdown.

Type: Leading.

Lesson: An analogy is not a signal; it points both ways unless a state variable locates you within it. Valuation warns of fragility; it does not time it.

5.4  COVID crash and recovery, 2020–2021

Observation: The fastest crash on record, followed by an unprecedented policy response.

The two branches: Wrong — a 2008-style depression, stay defensive. Right — the policy backstop produces a sharp recovery; buy risk, led by long-duration growth.

Discriminator available then: Two coincident ones. Credit and volatility marked the bottom — VIX peaked 16 March, credit and stocks bottomed 23 March, the day of the Fed's corporate-credit backstop. And real rates governed the later rotation — rising real yields favored value.

Outcome: S&P 500 fell 34% peak-to-trough, bottomed 23 March 2020, and recovered its highs by August. Growth led through 2020; vaccine news in November rotated leadership to value and energy (+93% by mid-2021).

Type: Coincident.

Lesson: The bottom cannot be forecast, only traded on sight. The fast reversal of the credit signal was itself the verdict that the down-move would not last.

5.5  Bear market and AI recovery, 2022–2024

Observation: Inflation surged and the Fed hiked at the fastest pace in 40 years; real rates rose from about −1.0% to +1.7%.

The two branches: Naive — buy the dip in growth. Right — rising real rates crush long-duration assets; own value, energy, short-duration, cash.

Discriminator available then: Two in sequence. For 2022: the 10-year real yield, rising from −1% to +2%, the primary driver of long-duration pricing. For 2023: a coherent cluster of AI-exposed names showing persistent unexplained return — a new driver the rate model could not capture, confirmed by earnings rather than just price.

Outcome: In 2022 the macro thesis worked cleanly: Nasdaq-100 −33%, long bonds −31%, energy +64%. In 2023 real rates stayed roughly flat yet big tech rose 55% and semis +73% — the macro signal was valid but a new driver overrode it.

Type: Coincident (real rates); new-driver detection (AI cluster).

Lesson: A coincident macro signal captures the known factors, not a new one. Detect new drivers as unexplained, clustered, persistent returns, and confirm them with earnings — the same test that separates the 2000 bubble (no profits) from 2024 (real profits).

5.6  Internet bubble and bust, 1998–2003

Observation: Internet and technology stocks went parabolic on a “new economy” story.

The two branches: Bullish — a genuine new economy, buy the leaders. Bearish — a bubble; valuations were the highest on record.

Discriminator available then: Valuation, plus the earnings gate — the 1999 leaders had real price gains but no earnings, the mark of a mania (the same gate that, in reverse, validated AI in 2023). The yield curve also inverted in April 2000, about a year before the recession.

Outcome: Nasdaq-100 fell 83% from its March 2000 peak to October 2002; S&P 500 fell 49%. The Fed cut rates from 6.5% to 1.75% through 2001 — the market kept falling for 18 more months.

Type: Leading.

Lesson: The valuation signal was correct but early by about three years and a further doubling of the index. The rate-cut signal that bottoms a liquidity crisis did not bottom this valuation unwind — the same signal means different things in different kinds of break. The lens classified the episode correctly but offered no way to time the top.

5.7  Subprime crisis, 2006–2009

Observation: A housing and credit boom, with equity indices making new highs into October 2007.

The two branches: Benign — a soft landing, stay invested. Right — hidden leverage in mortgage credit; a banking and funding crisis is building.

Discriminator available then: Not the usual equity signals — valuation was high but not extreme, and broad credit spreads stayed tight until mid-2007. The real ones were elsewhere: falling home prices, bank funding stress (the TED spread), and a cluster of sectors falling well before the index. The yield curve had also inverted in early 2006.

Outcome: Homebuilders peaked mid-2005 and fell roughly 75%; financials fell 80%. The S&P 500 peaked October 2007 and fell 57% to March 2009. The TED spread peaked above 4.5% in October 2008; its subsequent fall confirmed the bottom.

Type: Mixed — the curve and the sector cluster were leading; funding stress was coincident.

Lesson: The downside cluster located the crisis sector one to two years early, the curve gave an early warning, and funding stress marked both onset and bottom. But the crisis originated in markets the lens does not watch — mortgage credit, structured finance, bank funding — invisible until it transmitted to stocks in late 2007. The early warnings existed and were findable, but only if the observer looks beyond equities and rates.

7. What the examples establish

  1. Frame every regime call as observation, then competing branches, then the discriminator that decides between them. Finding the discriminator is the work; noticing the observation is not.
  2. Tag each discriminator by type. Coincident signals (themselves traded prices) are triggers — fast, full-size, quick to expire. Leading signals (slow structural states) are biases — gradual, partial, early. The recurring mistake is using a leading signal as a trigger (too early) or waiting for a coincident signal to confirm (too late).
  3. Durability can be checked, not forecast. A coincident signal can confirm a move is genuine (independent markets agreeing) and be monitored to see if it persists; lasting persistence requires a slow structural cause behind it.
  4. New drivers are not forecastable but are detectable, as coherent persistent returns the model cannot explain; an earnings check separates a real driver from a mania. The same detector run on the downside finds emerging risks (housing in 2007) — but only if it scans every cluster, including markets outside equities.
  5. Discriminator reliability depends on the kind of crisis. A coincident liquidity signal (rate cuts, funding spreads) bottoms a liquidity crisis (2009, 2020) but not a valuation unwind (2000–02). The system must know which kind of break it is in before trusting a given signal.
  6. A fitted statistical indicator is backward-looking — it recognizes past crisis types and confirms rather than predicts. Working in causes and discriminators, not a fitted score, is what lets the system face a crisis it has not seen.

8. What this means for the engine

The system has five parts:

  • Observer — measures the discriminators: rates, credit, the curve, valuation, volatility, funding stress, and the model's own unexplained residuals, across asset classes (not equities alone).
  • Thesis layer — holds the observation/branches/discriminator entries, each tagged coincident or leading, with a data source, a crisis-type condition, and an expiry rule.
  • Strategy builder — turns a live thesis into concrete positions.
  • Allocator — sizes by signal type: full and fast on coincident, partial and patient on leading.
  • Lifecycle manager — retires a thesis when its discriminator expires (a coincident reversal, a level crossed, a decaying correlation), not when it is merely in drawdown.

Where the difficulty sits: not in noticing regimes — everyone sees zero rates or rising rates — but in holding the discriminating variable consistently, sizing by signal type, timing the expiry, looking beyond the markets you usually watch, and finding the judgment to believe a newly detected driver. The first items are largely mechanical. The last is not, and is where a person or a reasoning model still adds the most.

9. Limitations

  • Seven episodes is a small sample, and the statistics within them use short windows.
  • Some discriminators need data outside the equity database — rates, inflation expectations, the curve, and corporate credit are available from public sources back decades (a credit spread reaches 1919); analyst earnings revisions are not, and would have to be bought. Daily cross-asset history is only robust from about 1990; before that, only monthly credit, curve, and valuation reach back to mid-century, and annual data (Shiller from 1871, Dimson from 1900) is useful for calibration, not for timing.
  • The final judgment on whether a newly detected driver is durable cannot be reduced to a formula. The machine can narrow the search and confirm earnings; it cannot decide that a technology will last.
